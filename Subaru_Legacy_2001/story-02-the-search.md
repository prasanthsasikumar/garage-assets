# The Search & Purchase

## Throwback

The obsession started before I ever moved to New Zealand, back when I knew nothing about cars except that I wanted one. The Nissan Z series pulled me in first, but the fixation soon narrowed to one very specific machine: the Subaru Legacy Twin Turbo, Rev C, the last revision to run that layout. Twin-turbo means two turbochargers taking turns force-feeding the engine, a gloriously overcomplicated arrangement that Subaru perfected and then quietly abandoned.

Budget was the problem. A good Legacy cost real money, and I had a student's version of none. The plan became finding a usable one for around 2,000 NZD, which meant months of talking to people, lurking on Subaru forums, and searching TradeMe with the persistence of a cron job.

The break came from a hackathon-style event that paid out 1,000 dollars in prize money. Not long after, a listing appeared at 2,700. I called the seller, a very cool British lady, and we settled on 2,500. It was cheap for what it was, and nothing seemed seriously wrong with the car. It simply wasn't running, because it had no battery.

Pickup day delivered the first lesson of Legacy ownership: "just needs a battery" is a diagnosis, not a promise. The car had sat so long that everything felt seized, and a jump-start did nothing. The right battery would have sorted it on the spot, but battery sizing was still a complete mystery to me then.

So I asked my good friend and flatmate Han to help fetch it. He had no NZ licence and wasn't comfortable driving on the left, being from China, but the plan was a short tow on quiet back roads. I took my little Integra, bought a tow cable from The Warehouse, and we collected the Legacy, still refusing to start. Then the navigation showed the highway was faster, so the highway is where we went. Han had to manage a dead car from behind: no engine running, which means no power steering and no power brakes. It was brutal. We were friends before that tow and brothers after it, and we found him a car of his own not long after.

Halfway home we stopped at SuperCheap Auto and bought the battery. After some fiddling the engine finally caught, and the gearbox freed itself in stages: reverse first, then the rest. On the drive back from SuperCheap the turbos spooled for the first time, and that sound was the hook.

The car clearly needed love: a cracked windscreen, a timing belt long overdue, ancient fluids, tired tyres, a dead stereo. I had no garage, but my good friend Amit had a garage and no car. Perfect trade. The Legacy moved to his place, and the restoration began.
