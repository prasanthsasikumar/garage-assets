# Subaru Legacy (2001)

## One of the Forever Cars

Some cars pass through your life. This one dug in.

On paper it is a 2001 Subaru Legacy, a sensible Japanese family wagon hiding two turbochargers under the bonnet. In practice it is the car I taught myself mechanics on, the car thieves tried twice to take from me, the car that carried my parents to Milford Sound and me through a PhD, and the one thing I refused to sell when I left New Zealand and sold everything else I owned. Little did I know, when I first got the car, how much of my life it would end up holding.

### Throwback
The obsession started before I ever moved to New Zealand, back when I knew nothing about cars except that I wanted one. The Nissan Z series pulled me in first, but the fixation soon narrowed to one very specific machine: the Subaru Legacy Twin Turbo, Rev C, the last revision to run that layout. Twin-turbo means two turbochargers taking turns force-feeding the engine, a gloriously overcomplicated arrangement that Subaru perfected and then quietly abandoned.

Budget was the problem. A good Legacy cost real money, and I had a student's version of none. The plan became finding a usable one for around 2,000 NZD, which meant months of talking to people, lurking on Subaru forums, and searching TradeMe with the persistence of a cron job.

The break came from a hackathon-style event that paid out 1,000 dollars in prize money. Not long after, a listing appeared at 2,700. I called the seller, a very cool British lady, and we settled on 2,500. It was cheap for what it was, and nothing seemed seriously wrong with the car. It simply wasn't running, because it had no battery.

Pickup day delivered the first lesson of Legacy ownership: "just needs a battery" is a diagnosis, not a promise. The car had sat so long that everything felt seized, and a jump-start did nothing. The right battery would have sorted it on the spot, but battery sizing was still a complete mystery to me then.

So I asked my good friend and flatmate Han to help fetch it. He had no NZ licence and wasn't comfortable driving on the left, being from China, but the plan was a short tow on quiet back roads. I took my little Integra, bought a tow cable from The Warehouse, and we collected the Legacy, still refusing to start. Then the navigation showed the highway was faster, so the highway is where we went. Han had to manage a dead car from behind: no engine running, which means no power steering and no power brakes. It was brutal. We were friends before that tow and brothers after it, and we found him a car of his own not long after.

Halfway home we stopped at SuperCheap Auto and bought the battery. After some fiddling the engine finally caught, and the gearbox freed itself in stages: reverse first, then the rest. On the drive back from SuperCheap the turbos spooled for the first time, and that sound was the hook.

The car clearly needed love: a cracked windscreen, a timing belt long overdue, ancient fluids, tired tyres, a dead stereo. I had no garage, but my good friend Amit had a garage and no car. Perfect trade. The Legacy moved to his place, and the restoration began.

### The Restoration
This was the biggest project I had ever taken on. My entire mechanical CV at that point was one oil change. So I treated the car the way I treat any hard problem: I read everything. I printed every Legacy manual I could find in English and translated the Japanese ones, and for the next two weeks I did nothing but read and watch YouTube. If something had been written down about maintaining a second-generation Legacy, chances are I read it.

The cam belt service was long overdue, and a snapped cam belt on this engine means the engine eats itself. I decided to do the job myself. I bought every component it needed and spent every last penny I had; I genuinely did not know how I would eat the following week, but I had the car parts. Then I started slowly, removing screws and labelling them meticulously, torquing every bolt that needed it and checking each one three times to be sure the setting was right.

### Acts of Kindness
Somewhere in the middle of the job, I hit a wall. To fit the cam belt I needed to lock the camshafts in place, and that called for a special tool Subaru makes, far beyond what I could afford. Searching and posting online only confirmed it: everyone said you need the tool. So I decided to walk into a workshop, offer to pay to rent one, and ask for help.

I went to Stubaru, a Subaru specialist in Christchurch that Stu runs with his wife. Instead of turning away a broke student, he patiently explained that I didn't actually need the factory tool at all: a 100-dollar tool from a shop nearby would do the trick. He even wrote me a note to take with me. I bought it, went back to the garage, and it worked.

I grew up in India, where people are kind, but I was never used to strangers going out of their way like that. That day changed something in me. I try to be that person now whenever someone comes to me for help. Stu doesn't know it, but he made a huge impact on my life. And it kept happening; having travelled a decent amount since, I can say the kindest people live down under in Aotearoa.

### First Drive
I still remember the first time I keyed the ignition. I had disabled the fuel pump to prime the engine, and the moment I reconnected it, the car fired straight up. The drive to the fuel station was spectacular. I had never driven anything with that much power and character, and I could not quite believe that everything I had done actually worked.

The next night I took it out properly. The car had no Warrant of Fitness or registration yet, so I waited for dark, when fewer police would be around. On an empty stretch I floored it and felt the turbos spool one after the other, and the speedo passed 180 before I had finished processing what was happening. I had never gone that fast in my life. I grinned the whole way home. Those moments laid the foundation for every project that came after.

The sensible bits followed: a new windscreen, a few minor fixes, and a passed warrant. Then the fun bits. The car came along on adventures to most corners of the South Island. I fitted boost gauges, tried different blow-off valves chasing that perfect "stuu tuuu" between gears, and replaced the exhaust. Later, once I finally had some money, I started buying other Legacies whenever they came up cheap, purely to swap the interesting bits across to this one. The car had stopped being a purchase and become a platform.

### Break-in Incident
The Subaru Legacy is one of the most stolen cars in New Zealand. It is unassuming, it has enough power to outrun trouble, and above all it is famously easy to steal. I knew all this when I bought one. You always know, right up until it happens to you.

Someone had been watching the car for a while. They had studied my routine at the lab: home for dinner in the evening, back for late nights through the winter months. One evening I came in at my usual time, felt unwell about ten minutes in, and decided to head home early. In the car park there were components lying on the ground beside the car, and the door was unlocked. Inside, the ignition barrel was smashed apart and a fistful of wires had been cut. The car had a highly rated alarm; somehow they had got past it.

What probably saved the Legacy is a safety switch tucked under the clutch that only triggers on a very hard press. They seem to have missed it, and spent their time rewiring things instead until they gave up, or until I nearly walked in on them. I called my friends at the lab and we towed the disabled car home. Over the following days I went to Pick-A-Part for a replacement ignition barrel and learned how to extract one from a donor car, which involves chiselling away at a security screw designed specifically to stop people doing what I was doing. Then I rewired the damaged loom and got the car running again.

### Trip with Parents
My family came to visit me in Christchurch, and the Legacy got its most important assignment yet. We drove from Christchurch to Queenstown past the impossible blue of Lake Tekapo, stayed in Arrowtown and Queenstown, and carried on to Milford Sound, coming home the long way through Dunedin. There were many trips in this car, but showing my parents a country so different from the one they raised me in, from the driver's seat of a car I had rebuilt with my own hands, was a big deal.

That trip was one role of many. Over those months the Legacy was the family car, the camper, the beach car, the fishing vehicle, the tow rig, and the roadside assist for half my friends. Whatever the plan was, the car was the main player in it.

### Life in Wellington
In 2018 I moved to Wellington for work, on a contract with Te Papa, the national museum of New Zealand. When the contract ended, the income ended with it. Job applications went nowhere, so I stacked up whatever work I could find, including a stint at PlaceMakers, and slowly paid off my debts. Before that gig came through, though, there was a stretch with no money at all. Even at the peak of it, genuinely hungry, I did not sell the car. I kept telling myself the rough patch would pass and the Legacy would still be there on the other side of it. It was. That era is also when the car finally got the BBS wheels I had always wanted; upgrades happened whenever I could afford them, which was rarely, which made each one sweeter.

### Stashing the Car Back in Christchurch
Then Wellington handed me the opportunity of a lifetime: an offer to do a PhD with Mark Billinghurst, the father of Virtual Reality. I took it without hesitation. I was still broke, and keeping a car in Auckland was out of the question, so a friend offered their place in Christchurch as parking while I sorted my life out. I drove the Legacy down, said my goodbyes, and flew north. Life got busy the way a PhD makes sure it does, and the car stayed in the South Island for about a year. I made it back once, just long enough to start it and move it around. There was another break-in attempt during that time too, but all they took was the boost gauge. The car, patient as ever, waited.

### Moving to Auckland
Right after Covid I was finally able to bring the car up from Christchurch to Auckland. By then our place had about eleven cars parked at it at any given time, and the neighbours had started to wonder what our real jobs were. The Legacy went back to being the daily driver, and life was good: late-night drives, car meetups, and the small milestone of affording Pirelli tyres for the first time. Used ones, but Pirellis.

The trouble with daily driving a car you love on a PhD budget is that the car keeps needing things the budget cannot supply. Services started slipping. A sound began coming from the gearbox now and then, and I could feel a faint binding through the driveline in certain corners. I never did the inspection. Why fix something that is not broken, the saying goes. That saying was about to cost me, and set up one of my greatest stories yet.

### Epic Breakdown
I had just finished my PYR, the milestone in a New Zealand PhD where you present your work to a committee that decides whether you get to continue. Naturally, I decided this called for a holiday. The plan was short and simple: a day at the beach, then camping at my favourite spot near Rotorua, then on to Tauranga and home. It went well until Rotorua, with me babying the car every time I felt that familiar binding. In hindsight, the front differential was probably already running dry.

On the way from Rotorua to Tauranga I skipped the normal road and took the logging route instead, because I had recently passed a camper van wearing the slogan "choose the scenic route, especially when lost", and it had clearly got into my head. It was a beautiful drive, right up until I forgot I was supposed to be babying the car. In a dip on a curve, the differential let go with a loud burst, and the Legacy lost all drive. I got it to the side of the road, opened the bonnet, put the hazards on, and could do nothing else. Log trucks kept appearing behind me in my lane with very little time to react; fully loaded, they cannot stop, and they were missing the car by not much.

While I was stuck there, a group of Māori who spoke no English at all passed by in the other lane, took a U-turn, and came back for me. I pulled out my tow rope and hooked it to their SUV, and they understood the assignment immediately, towing me up to a stretch with a proper shoulder where the car could sit in safety. We could barely exchange a sentence, but they gave me exactly the help I needed most. Once I was safe, they said their goodbyes and carried on.

There was no phone signal in the area, so I climbed the hill nearby until I found some, called my friend Reza, and WhatsApped him my GPS coordinates to pass on to Mohsen, our tow truck guy. Then I climbed down, waited, and climbed up again later to confirm: message received, Mohsen was coming. The breakdown had happened around 11 in the morning, and Mohsen needed five hours to reach me, so I settled in with the food, water and books I had in the car. All afternoon, people stopped to ask if I needed anything: food, water, a lift to the nearest town. Even the police came by and radioed my situation through to their colleagues so everyone on that road knew someone was stranded there.

Mohsen arrived around 8 in the evening. We loaded the car onto the truck and began the long haul back to Auckland. This is a man who drove ten hours so that I could be home, and then refused to accept any money for it. Don't worry, I paid him later. The sheer kindness of people is something I kept running into over and over in New Zealand, and that day on the logging road concentrated all of it into a few hours. Mohsen is an absolute legend.

### Keeping the Car in Storage
As the PhD wound down, I decided to leave New Zealand and go see the world. That meant selling everything. And I mean everything: the engine cranes, the stands, the compressors, the spare engines, the multiple copies of most tools that years of projects had accumulated. One by one it all went. The only thing I chose to keep was the Legacy.

I found a storage spot out in Hamilton, about an hour from Auckland. I cleaned the car up properly, drove it to Mohsen's house with a heavy heart, and we loaded it onto his truck one last time and took it to where it sits now. Saying goodbye is always hard, and it is hardest when you don't know how long it will be until you meet again. There is a little underbody rust starting to show, and I hope it holds off. My hope is to earn enough to ship the car to whichever country I land in, restore it to its former glory, and make it my daily once more.

### What is in for the Future?
Future plans are always a mystery, but this one is fully specced out in my head. The dream is to move to the US someday, base my company there, and import the Legacy under the vintage vehicle rule; as a 2001 model, it qualifies in 2026. The build would be a full strip, sandblast and repaint, either the original colour or grey, plus a Rev C body kit and a built engine making around 450 horsepower, reliably. But the twin-turbo setup stays, valley of death and all: that strange dead moment while the small turbo hands over to the big one, the flaw that gives the car its character. Everyone else engineers their quirks out. This car keeps every single one.
