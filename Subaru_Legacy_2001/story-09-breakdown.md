# Epic Breakdown

I had just finished my PYR, the milestone in a New Zealand PhD where you present your work to a committee that decides whether you get to continue. Naturally, I decided this called for a holiday. The plan was short and simple: a day at the beach, then camping at my favourite spot near Rotorua, then on to Tauranga and home. It went well until Rotorua, with me babying the car every time I felt that familiar binding. In hindsight, the front differential was probably already running dry.

On the way from Rotorua to Tauranga I skipped the normal road and took the logging route instead, because I had recently passed a camper van wearing the slogan "choose the scenic route, especially when lost", and it had clearly got into my head. It was a beautiful drive, right up until I forgot I was supposed to be babying the car. In a dip on a curve, the differential let go with a loud burst, and the Legacy lost all drive. I got it to the side of the road, opened the bonnet, put the hazards on, and could do nothing else. Log trucks kept appearing behind me in my lane with very little time to react; fully loaded, they cannot stop, and they were missing the car by not much.

While I was stuck there, a group of Māori who spoke no English at all passed by in the other lane, took a U-turn, and came back for me. I pulled out my tow rope and hooked it to their SUV, and they understood the assignment immediately, towing me up to a stretch with a proper shoulder where the car could sit in safety. We could barely exchange a sentence, but they gave me exactly the help I needed most. Once I was safe, they said their goodbyes and carried on.

There was no phone signal in the area, so I climbed the hill nearby until I found some, called my friend Reza, and WhatsApped him my GPS coordinates to pass on to Mohsen, our tow truck guy. Then I climbed down, waited, and climbed up again later to confirm: message received, Mohsen was coming. The breakdown had happened around 11 in the morning, and Mohsen needed five hours to reach me, so I settled in with the food, water and books I had in the car. All afternoon, people stopped to ask if I needed anything: food, water, a lift to the nearest town. Even the police came by and radioed my situation through to their colleagues so everyone on that road knew someone was stranded there.

Mohsen arrived around 8 in the evening. We loaded the car onto the truck and began the long haul back to Auckland. This is a man who drove ten hours so that I could be home, and then refused to accept any money for it. Don't worry, I paid him later. The sheer kindness of people is something I kept running into over and over in New Zealand, and that day on the logging road concentrated all of it into a few hours. Mohsen is an absolute legend.
