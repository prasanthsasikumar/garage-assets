# Subaru Forester (2003)

## Learning the Hard Way

Few cars taught me as much as this Subaru Forester. It began as one of those impulsive recoveries that seem manageable in theory and far more complicated in reality. I went to collect it without arranging a tow truck in advance, and the moment I got it out of the yard it started pouring out thick white smoke. That immediately changed the plan. I had it towed to a friend's apartment car park because I did not even have a place to store it properly at the time.

It soon became clear that the turbocharger had failed. I sourced a used replacement and set about fitting it in the car park itself, working with whatever tools, space, and patience I could muster. In the process, I made a costly mistake: before the first start-up, I forgot to prime the engine properly, which damaged that replacement turbo as well. It was the kind of error that only stays with you because you never repeat it.

At the same time, life was moving quickly around the car. I had to change houses, and somehow managed to drive the Forester across town in the middle of the night to the new place before finishing the repair properly there. Once sorted, though, it became an excellent car to have around. It was genuinely enjoyable to drive, practical for carrying things, and especially valuable during the lockdown period. Just before lockdown, I had fitted a towbar, which made it even more useful while moving household items and settling into a new home.

In the end, I sold the Forester because I needed the money for a MacBook. Even so, it stayed important in another way: it became the car in which Ryo first learned to drive, which is part of why another Forester later entered the family. This one was not the easiest or the cheapest car I have owned, but it was one of the most educational. It forced me to learn, improvise, make mistakes, and keep going.
