# Honda Integra (2017)

## First Car — Made Me Realize That Cars Can Actually Make Money

I was recovering from ACL surgery and getting around by bicycle or on foot was becoming genuinely difficult. The moment I found myself struggling down the road carrying 10 kg of rice from an Indian grocery store — my first month in New Zealand — I decided I needed a car. I found a beater on TradeMe for $650 NZD and bought it without much deliberation. In the worst case, it would last a few months. It ended up lasting considerably longer.

It was freedom. One afternoon I locked myself out with the keys still in the ignition — at the HIT Lab NZ car park where I was doing my masters. I called the AA, found out an unlock would cost $300, and decided I would figure it out myself. I searched online, borrowed a few tools from the lab, and got back in through the frameless door design without causing any damage. That was the first DIY. Ironically, this is the same car park where my Legacy was broken into a few months later.

From early on I had been reading about cars and engines. I was particularly drawn to the Nissan Z series and anything with a turbocharger — twin-turbo setups especially. I kept that in mind as I drove the Integra and kept learning.

One Sunday I sourced secondhand oil change equipment and picked up a $15 toolbox from The Warehouse, then serviced the car myself. I did this without properly knowing the jack points or technique. Looking back it was reckless, but it was a start, and starting is what matters.

The Integra taught me what a Warrant of Fitness involves, how the registration system works, and what it actually means to own and maintain a vehicle. I drove on my Indian licence for the first year as permitted. When I eventually sold it, I came away with roughly $200 more than I had paid. It was not much — but the realisation that a car could make money rather than just cost money changed everything. This was where it all began.
